How fast can evolution work?

Posted 25 January 2007 by Mike Dunford

There's been a bit of talk about "Evolutionary Speed Limits" over at the Intelligent Design weblog Uncommon Descent. Most of the discussion involves "Haldane's Dilemma." This concept is rooted in an article written by the noted evolutionary geneticist J. B. S Haldane in 1957. There's a lot of math involved, and you can see it over at the Wikipedia page I linked above. The bottom line, for those not interested in the math, is this: according to Haldane's calculations, a species cannot reasonably fix beneficial mutations (a particular mutation becomes "fixed" when it is present in all of the population) at a rate any faster than 1 mutation per 300 generations.

A number of anti-evolutionists have taken this as evidence against evolution. If, they argue, genetic changes can only be fixed at a rate of 1 per 300 generations, how can evolution possibly explain the differences between species like humans and chimps, where not nearly enough generations have passed to account for the number of differences that we observe. There are a number of problems with using Haldane's calculations in this way, and in this post I'm going to look at one of those - the one that I think is the most important. For clarity, I should probably make sure that I am very explicit about what, exactly, the problem is before I start, so here it is:

Using Haldane's 1 substitution per 300 generations as a speed limit for all evolution is wrong because Haldane's calculations and concerns only apply under certain very specific circumstances.

Even granting all of the creationists assumptions for Haldane's dilemma, we would expect plenty of time and mutations to explain the difference between humans and chimpanzees.

Assume 1 mutation fixed by drastic selection per 300 generations,15 years per generation, and 6 million years since humans and chimps last had a common ancestor. This give a value of approximately 1,300 possible human mutations strongly selected by the environment. Further assume that chimps have been evolving at similar rate away from our last common ancestor, and that they too have accumulated 1,300 mutations. The combined differences allowed by the creationist caricature of Haldane is 2,600 genes, or nearly 10% of all genes.

Strongly selected differences in 10% of our genes should be more than enough to distinguish us from our nearest relatives, given the drastic effects that even one nucleotide change can have on an organism.
Nevertheless, we surely differ from chimps by more than one nucleotide in one-tenth of our genes. If the 10% of strongly selected genes are distributed more or less at random, then very many non-selected mutations would be dragged along by linkage to the selected genes.

This argument doesn't even address what the creationists got wrong about Haldane's dilemma. Using their own misguided arguments (read lies for those cretins who really know better, there has been plenty of time for evolution to work exactly as the theory predicts.
