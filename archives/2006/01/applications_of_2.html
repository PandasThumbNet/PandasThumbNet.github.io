Applications of Evolution 3 - tradeoffs in resistance.

Posted 11 January 2006 by Mike Dunford

Someone just emailed me a copy of an interesting press release. Some time back, a particular mutation known as CCR5delta32 was identified as conferring greatly increased resistance to HIV in individuals who had two copies of that particular gene (in geek terms, those are individuals homozygous for that particular allele). According to the press release, a group of researchers have discovered that this resistance to HIV comes with a price. The individuals who are homozygous for the CCR5delta32 allele do have greatly increased resistance to HIV, but they also have greatly decreased resistance to the West Nile Virus. Joseph O'Donnell · 13 January 2006

CCR5 is a deletion of a receptor that is normally involved in detecting molecules the immune system uses called Chemokines. It's typically displayed on macrophages and dendritic cells and some T-cells. It basically allows cells of the immune system to follow a chemical gradient around and find their way to sites of interest around your body. When CCR5 is missing, it is not terribly detrimental because there are numerous other chemokine receptors that can maintain enough function so the loss isn't significant.
