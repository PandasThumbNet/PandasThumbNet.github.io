Suggest Google News Sources

Posted 1 July 2006 by Wesley R. Elsberry

Those of you who use Google News Alerts for phrases like "intelligent design" and "evolutionary biology" have probably noticed that the Discovery Institute's Media Complaints Division (aka "Evolution News and Views") turns up with depressing regularity. More recently, I was amazed to find that "Uncommon Descent" weblog posts were also being treated as news sources. So, it seemed to me that it couldn't be so difficult to get a source listed in Google News. And it wasn't. It is in the Google News FAQ:

What if I don't see my favorite news source in Google News? We're as shocked as you are! If we're missing a publisher that we should be covering, please send us your ideas. While we can't guarantee that we'll heed your recommendation, we do promise to review all the suggestions we receive without regard to political viewpoint or ideology.

I sent in a suggestion that they pick up the National Center for Science Education main page as a news source, and within a week got notice that they were adding it to their list. Recent News Alerts have included NCSE front page items, so that particular suggestion is complete. Now that you know how it is done and that it works, I'd like to ask the PT readership to take a moment to nominate various pro-science sources to the fine folks at Google News. You may find the list here on the PT right-hand sidebar useful to find candidates. Check out the links at the TalkOrigins Archive, too. Please leave a message in the comments for each site that you do nominate.

Keanus · 2 July 2006

Mephisto's concerns are well placed, but things will get better in the decades to come. I edited and published K-12 science text books from the 1960's until the '90's. From shortly after the Scopes trial until the 1960's American pre-college biology (high school) and life science (junior high school) texts almost uniformly omitted the word evolution. A few touched on the subject, without the name, of course, but most did not even cover it. Then along came the BSCS (Biological Science Curriculum Study), funded by the federal government, which made evolution the centerpiece of their three basal and one AP text. The straight commercial publisher slowly began to incorporate evolution into their texts. That occurred almost concurrently with the various suits against uncooperative state government who sought to keep evolution out of the classroom. The two developments drew strength from each other.

Nonetheless the creationists had power and weren't shy about using it. I can recall as late as 1975 enduring strong pressure from our field sales force to downplay evolution or put it in a chapter by itself so teachers could skip it. Editors and publishers for other publishing companies felt the same pressure.

In any event the result that it's a near certainty that anyone who passed through junior high and high school before 1970 in the US was exposed to almost no evolution at all. That gave the country a couple of generations of people, among which is Dubya and his closest advisers, with absolutely no background in evolution. But even in the years since the early seventies, although the textbooks mostly cover evolution and cover it reasonably well, many classroom teachers are pressured locally to pass over it completely or finesse the topic with little more than a class or two. Few have the guts of Pat New in Dahlonega, Georgia, to stand up to the pressure. The job, their family, and peace in the community are often more important.

But despite the best efforts of the DI and its pooh-bahs of PR, things will get better slowly and just maybe in fifty years or so American society will catch up with the rest of the developed world on this issue. In the meantime we need to keep the pressure up on state standards, text book publishers, and politicians to be sure that any back sliding is brief, very brief.
