Jim Loter · 20 April 2008

IANAL, but I work with the Fair Use doctrine in an educational context. The amount of a work used is but one of four factors determining fair use. Perhaps the most important factor is the commercial or non-commercial nature of the referencing work. "Expelled" is a commercial film. Another factor is the nature of the copyrighted work itself. "Imagine" is a commercial work, too. So you have a situation where a commercial work is using another commercial work without permission. That's why TV commercials get permission to use brief clips of songs.

The fourth factor is "the effect of the use upon the potential market for or value of the copyrighted work," and I think it should be pretty easy to demonstrate that the effect in this case is "negative."

The length of the work used would probably be considered completely irrelevant in light of the other infringing factors.

Neil, you make an interesting comment (emphasis mine):

Do you know something the rest of us don't know? Do tell!

The press release throughout says "use of THE SONG", song of course including words and music, which is where I as a musician get off the Fair Use train, in this particular case.

I'll admit they *may* have rights to the words under Fair Use laws, but I absolutely vehemently with all my heart and soul know that to call use of THE MUSIC fair, in circumstances such as this, would overturn the very thing Fair Use is there for. It's supposed to be to protect artists to be able to create new works of art.

If uses like this, which are commonplace in both documentary and fiction film, are called Fair Use, then it's a blanket allowance for documentary filmmakers to use, for free, essentially any recorded music they want to, and to never have to hire a composer to create new music. This results in ... what was it Fair Use law was supposed to protect? Oh, that's right, the abilities of artists (composers) to be able to create new works of art (new original music for documentary films, in this case).
