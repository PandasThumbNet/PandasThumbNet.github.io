Science blogs: ur doin it wrong.

Posted 8 March 2010 by Richard B. Hoppe

An open access paper just out looks at science blogging. According to the abstract, the paper
... focuses on one of the ICTs [Information and Communication Technologies] that have already been adopted in science communication, on science blogging. The findings from the analysis of eleven blogs are presented in an attempt to understand current practices of science blogging and to provide insight into the role of blogging in the promotion of more interactive forms of science communication.
Bora has a critical look at it, as does Cosmic Variance. Panda's Thumb is one of the 11 blogs examined in the paper. One of the main conclusions of the (pretty chancy) analysis is that
To become a tool for non-scientist participation, science blogs need to stabilize as a genre or as a set of subgenres where smaller conversations may facilitate more meaningful participation from members of the public. Science bloggers need to become more aware of their audience, welcome non-scientists, and focus on explanatory, interpretative, and critical modes of communication rather than on reporting and opinionating.
The author goes on to suggest that
An interesting practical experiment would also be to reverse the roles of writers and readers and invite the so called "ordinary persons" to create and publish science blogs, i.e., to engage them in the practices of science blog writing rather than reading or commenting.
Hm? Why would that be interesting? And, for that matter, "ordinary persons" have the same access to blogging software as do scientists; nothing (except disinclination or disinterest) is stopping "ordinary persons" from blogging about anything they wish. The author clearly has a particular model in mind as a referent, implicit in the title of the paper: "Science blogs and public engagement with science: practices, challenges, and opportunities." That's tantamount to "blogs as an extension of science education." But while many of us are interested in science education, that's an institutional goal while blogs are, by and large, personal vehicles. It seems to me that institutionalization is not a state to be desired. (After writing this paragraph, I found that Scholarly Kitchen made much the same point.) (I invite my PT colleagues to comment. This post is based on a fast read-through with contractors waiting to abduct me to force a decision on the color of house siding.)

Mike Elzinga · 9 March 2010

Blogging is a relatively recent phenomenon. Prior to that, the best “up-to-date” information about science was received passively from a relatively few science reporters who were both articulate and knowledgeable.

It was true when I was researching full time and it is still true today; the typical researcher spends 60 to 80 hour weeks just dealing with all the aspects of research and mentoring.

So most researchers simply would not have time to write articles for the layperson; and most would not be articulate enough nor would they understand the preconceptions and misconceptions many laypersons bring with them to their reading about science.

Blogging is more direct and interactive; but as we see with the trolls, it gets cluttered up with pages of pure nauseating crap that drives people away in exasperation.

But even with the directness and interactivity, most front-line researchers don’t have the time to spend mentoring laypersons in addition to all their other responsibilities. And it doesn’t count towards any kind of career advancement.

Retirees have often been excellent sources for educating the public. There have been a number of retirees who have produced a series of articles in science magazines and trade journals that have been warmly received (Dick Crane of the University of Michigan comes to mind).

I don’t know how many retirees care to run blogs however. I don’t know how many would necessarily know how or would have the energy. It appears to require a lot of time and site management.
