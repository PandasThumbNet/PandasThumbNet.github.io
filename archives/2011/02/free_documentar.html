As a shining example of censorship in science, I'm going to quote the opening paragraphs from the editorial in this week's Science News:

In the early days of Science News, editor Watson Davis compiled an extensive list of "stories that should be handled with care." By that he meant that they should be avoided altogether. Among the obvious prohibitions were stories about "telepathy and mind reading", claims of perpetual motion, and reports of sea serpents or "man-eating trees". Watson also issued warnings about divining rods, death rays and "messages from or to Mars or other planets." In the medical realm he cited claims of cures for cancer, baldness, obesity, and "cure of rabies by a stone or by shooting the dog."

So you see, the mainstream scientific publications have a priori decided to reject a lot of things without even any consideration of the merits (although Davis's fine print did allow for exceptions to all of these prohibitions, given valid scientific evidence).
